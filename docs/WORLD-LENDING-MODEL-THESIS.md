## 1. The thesis in one paragraph

Every lending institution today decides with a chain of **frozen-snapshot, single-target point predictors** — a scorecard, a PD/LGD/EAD stack, a rules engine — each fit to a static, selection-biased extract and stitched across lifecycle silos by workflow software and human hands. None of these objects, individually or together, is a model of *how the operation behaves when you act on it*. The defensible claim of this thesis is deliberately **architectural and deflationary, not a claim of modelling novelty**: the methods a lending operation needs to reason about its own dynamics — survival/hazard models, uplift/CATE estimation, contextual bandits and constrained RL for pricing and contact strategy, portfolio simulation — *already exist and are mostly off-the-shelf*. What does **not** exist anywhere today is the **substrate they require to work end-to-end**: a complete, attributed, single-ledger record of **every action, the choice set it was selected from, the reasoning behind it, and its realised outcome**, across origination → underwriting → servicing → collections → disputes → reporting. That substrate is the missing precondition, and it is **structurally** absent from incumbent stacks (their ledgers record transactions, not the reasoning or the rejected alternatives), from point tools (they see one slice), and from research systems (they learn in simulators, not inside a governed bank perimeter on real, gated actions). A **World Lending Model (WLM)** is the learned, action-conditioned model of a lending operation that this substrate *makes learnable at all* — and pre-execution validation (Krim-Nyāya) is what makes the runtime that produces the substrate safe enough to be allowed to run a regulated lender. **What is real now** is the substrate, the deny-by-default validation gate, per-tenant supervised models, and uncertainty-bounded near-term forward simulation on an existing book. **What is DIRECTION** is the high-fidelity simulating model, the counterfactual access engine, and the compounding federated flywheel — each gated not by ambition but by hard, named problems (causal identification, compounding rollout error, slow censored labels, performativity, fair-lending coverage) that this document confronts rather than waves at. The honest register: Krim is making an **architectural bet that is correct and early**, not claiming a moat or a shipped world model today.

## 2. What a World Lending Model is — a precise definition

A **world model**, in the technical sense, is a learned model of an environment's dynamics that, given a state and a candidate action, predicts the next state and outcome, and is used as a stand-in for the real environment so a planner can evaluate actions in imagination instead of by taking them (Sutton's Dyna, 1990–91; Ha & Schmidhuber, "World Models," arXiv:1803.10122, 2018). Formally, in a Markov decision process it approximates a transition model p(s′ | s, a) and an outcome/reward model and is queried with hypothetical actions.

A **World Lending Model** is this object with the state being a **lending operation**:

- **State** s_t — a borrower, a portfolio, a macro context, an operational position.
- **Action** a_t — approve at price p; set a limit; restructure; send a Reg-B adverse-action notice; schedule a collections contact within FDCPA hours; resolve a dispute.
- **Predicted next state and outcome** — cure, default, prepayment, complaint, recovery, and the resulting movement in portfolio risk — emitted **with calibrated uncertainty** that must widen off-distribution.

**One precise scoping point, load-bearing.** The strongest results in the field come from *value-/decision-relevant* models — MuZero (Schrittwieser et al., *Nature* 588, 2020) learned to plan without being given the rules, modelling only what mattered for the decision; the JEPA/world-model program (LeCun, "A Path Towards Autonomous Machine Intelligence," 2022) argues for predicting in representation space and discarding unpredictable surface detail. So a WLM should model **decision-relevant dynamics, not reconstruct a bank**. It sits on the **model-based-planning axis, not the generative-simulator axis** (DeepMind Genie, Fei-Fei Li's World Labs optimise perceptual fidelity — irrelevant here).

**Three corrections this version makes to its own earlier framing, because the red-team was right:**

1. **The MuZero/Dreamer/JEPA reference is a reference, not a pedigree.** Those systems succeeded in *stationary, perfect- or full-information, effectively infinite-sample, resettable* environments. Lending is *non-stationary, partially observed, adversarial, low-signal, slow-labelled, and has no reset button.* The analogy is useful for one thing only — establishing that "a learned action-conditioned model you can plan against" is a real scientific object with a real literature — and it is misleading for everything else. We therefore **do not call the WLM "MuZero for lending."** The honest description is: *a unified, attributed, action-conditioned operational dataset and runtime that finally makes existing causal / uplift / survival / constrained-RL methods applicable end-to-end, under a hard pre-execution safety gate.*

2. **"Action-conditioned" does not mean "counterfactual." This was a non-sequitur in v1 and is now retired.** Conditioning on actions that *appear in the training data* is not the same as identifying the effect of *intervening* with an action on a population you never sampled (Pearl's seeing-vs-doing). In reject-inference lending the counterfactual "what would the declined applicant have done if approved" is **point-unidentified** without either a credible exclusion restriction/instrument (rare and contestable in credit) or deliberate randomized variation. A neural dynamics model does not manufacture identification; it fits the observational distribution and then extrapolates into the never-approved region where there is, by construction, zero label support. The counterfactual capability is therefore **earned operationally** (see §6.1), not asserted from fitting.

3. **The right modelling stance is structured-prior-plus-learned-residual, not tabula-rasa learned latent dynamics.** Lending has decades of mechanistic structure (hazard/survival, PD/LGD/EAD, behavioural scoring, amortisation accounting). A WLM should be a *mostly-mechanistic model with learned residuals and learned policy-response terms*, which both cuts the sample requirement dramatically and keeps the object inside the explainability and model-risk regimes. This is the opposite of "let a latent-dynamics network learn the bank from scratch," and it is the realistic design.

So the WLM is best read as **a substrate claim with a modelling program attached**, not a new algorithm. That is the version that survives a scientist in the room.

## 3. The shift — from frozen-snapshot prediction to a simulating, compounding model

Today's lending decisioning is a **frozen-snapshot, siloed-substrate paradigm**. State the limit as *architectural*, not as "banks use dumb models" — sophisticated lenders already run gradient-boosted models, SHAP, trended data (FICO 10T, VantageScore 4.0/5.0) and uplift in collections, and re-estimate IFRS 9 / CECL provisioning each reporting period. The real, defensible critique is not staleness of any one model; it is the **absence of an operation-level, action-conditioned model and the loss of the data that would let one be built** (established fact for the architecture; the world-model-as-remedy framing is inference):

- The decision is a **chain of single-target models** (score → PD/LGD/EAD → fraud/affordability/propensity), each predicting **one** target, stitched together downstream, not learned jointly across the lifecycle.
- The dominant core-decision technology is still **logistic regression on Weight-of-Evidence-binned features**, chosen for ECOA reason-codes and SR 11-7 conceptual soundness — explainability, not predictive ceiling, sets the architecture.
- The score is only an input; the **approve/decline/price decision is a separate rules engine** (Blaze, PowerCurve, Provenir, ACTICO). Intelligence and policy are architecturally distinct.
- **LOS/LMS workflow software is the glue** (nCino, Blend, ICE/Encompass; FIS, Fiserv, Jack Henry, Finastra), each a different data model. The origination decision's *reasoning* does not flow into servicing; collections has no structured access to *why* underwriting approved on those terms.
- **Critically, no system records the rejected choice set.** Legacy stacks log the chosen action, not the alternatives considered and declined and why. A retrospective data-lake join can reconstruct (state, action-taken, outcome) for a single stage at best; it can almost never reconstruct (state, *choice-set-considered*, action-taken, *reasoning*, outcome) across the lifecycle. This is the genuinely subtle and genuinely missing thing (see §5).

Four pathologies follow (the first two are established fact; the framing of an operation-level model as the remedy is inference):

1. **Reject-inference / selection bias.** The scorecard sees outcomes only for *approved* applicants; it learns the bank's historical *policy*, not ground truth. This is an unobserved-counterfactual problem with known identification limits — and it is exactly where any learned model is *least* reliable, not most (the opposite of how it is usually pitched).
2. **Single-stage myopia.** An origination model optimising P(default) is blind to servicing cost and collections curability — it cannot see that a marginally riskier-but-curable borrower may be more profitable than a "safe" prepayer.
3. **Lost reasoning and lost alternatives.** Because neither the rationale nor the rejected choice set is recorded, the substrate for an action-conditioned model is never created in the first place.
4. **No operation-level simulation.** You cannot ask a scorecard a portfolio or sequence question; it returns one score for one applicant.

The shift is from a **set of fixed functions over a censored, siloed substrate** to **a unified, attributed, action-conditioned record over which dynamics methods become applicable** — and, downstream and as DIRECTION, a rollout-able model you can intervene on within hard validated constraints. The stakes are unusually high precisely because the off-distribution region (new rate regime, new fraud pattern) is where banks most want guidance and where *any* learned model is least trustworthy. That tension is not a footnote; it shapes what may be claimed (§6.2).

## 4. Why it could be transformative — the lifecycle walk, with mechanisms (and honest horizons)

The dual ROI is **one capability pointed two ways**: the same whole-operation, action-conditioned model that can responsibly extend credit at the margin is the structure that can move a whole-operation cost base. **Important register correction from v1:** the objective being optimised is the *lender's* risk-adjusted return, and fairness/affordability are not side-constraints traded off at the margin — they are **hard gates that block actions** (§6.5). And we **do not call cost-defence-versus-access "a mission."** Cost-defence is the honest buyer motive; access is a *second-order consequence that only materialises where affordability and Consumer-Duty gates bind*, and it is DIRECTION until borrower-welfare metrics are first-class measured outputs. The reviewer who called "access-unlock makes it a mission" the line that converts a defensible dual-use claim into an oversold social one was right; it is struck.

**Where the value is genuinely near-term and in-distribution (the part that does not depend on long-horizon rollouts):**

- **Whole-lifecycle, portfolio-aware decisioning.** An origination decision that *internalises* downstream servicing cost and collections curability is a real, near-distribution improvement that siloed point models structurally cannot make — independent of any 24-month rollout. The same applicant can price differently depending on whether they add or reduce correlation with the existing book.
- **Short-horizon sequence optimisation in servicing and collections.** "Which contact next, when, on which channel, or restructure" is a problem whose outcomes label in **weeks**, so rollout error is bounded and the trustworthy zone is real. This is where the **~12-touch, ~$15–$40-per-touch** loan economics (McKinsey, cited industry figure — HIGH-CONF as cited) actually move: not by deleting touches, but by learning which *sequence* produces the cure — *within* hard validated constraints (FDCPA contact hours, FCA Consumer Duty good-outcome tests, affordability).

**Where the value is DIRECTION and must be sold as uncertainty-bounded, never as prediction:**

- **Forward portfolio simulation / scenario exploration.** "What happens to this book over 24 months if I tighten DTI by three points" and "Monte-Carlo the book through many macro paths" are the *least* trustworthy uses, because per-step model error compounds over a rollout and finance is non-stationary — and a *policy change* is by definition a deviation from the data-collection policy, the regime where even flagship value-equivalent models are shown to evaluate poorly (He et al., 2024, on what MuZero actually learns). **v1 contradicted itself by leading with this in §4 and disclaiming it in §6; v2 resolves the contradiction in favour of the honest, narrower claim and uses it in both places.** The defensible pitch is *not* "see tail risk before it materialises." It is: *replace static, prescribed scenario overlays with a learned, continuously-recalibrated scenario engine whose predictive intervals widen with horizon and which is reconciled to realised outcomes every period via the one-ledger backtest.* That is a genuine improvement on a frozen annual re-fit **only if** the uncertainty quantification is honest and the marketing stops claiming point trajectories. It is also no worse than incumbent practice: CCAR/DFAST and IFRS 9/CECL scenario testing are *already* multi-year model-based extrapolation into regimes with no training support, accepted by regulators with documented limitations. The WLM's claim is a better-instrumented version of that, not a crystal ball.

**The access mechanism — stated honestly.** The IFC / SME Finance Forum estimates the MSME finance gap at roughly **$5.7T for formal MSMEs** (on the order of ~$8T including informal) across ~119 emerging markets (cited as an IFC figure — confirm report vintage before any external use). But the gap is **not one thing**, and v1 conflated two: (a) genuine *information friction* — creditworthy thin-file borrowers a frozen, reject-biased model cannot see; and (b) genuine *credit risk and non-arbitrary cost-to-serve* under thin-file, high-volatility, weak-enforcement, no-collateral conditions. A WLM can attack (a) — by lowering the marginal operating cost that makes small tickets uneconomic, and by extending an *uncertainty-bounded* risk estimate near the historical margin. It **cannot** dissolve (b) by model extrapolation, and "extend a defensible risk estimate to borrowers the frozen model never saw" must be qualified hard: the **deep never-approved population is not addressable by counterfactual fitting** and requires new data, partnerships, or alternative-data origination, not model confidence (§6.1). The honest access claim is *"safely serve more of the borrowers at the margin whom the cost base and the blind spot currently exclude, where the affordability gate binds,"* not *"unlock $5.7T."* The unqualified version is exactly the microfinance-over-indebtedness pattern (Andhra Pradesh 2010) in modern dress; "access" and "extraction" look identical in an NPV that does not measure borrower welfare.

**The defence mechanism.** Point automation cannot move a **~51% cost-to-income** base (McKinsey — HIGH-CONF as cited) or defend the **~$170B / ~9%** profit-pool erosion McKinsey projects over 5–10 years (card lending −34%, consumer deposits −27% sharpest — HIGH-CONF as cited). The reason a reported **~95% of enterprise GenAI pilots show no P&L return** (MIT NANDA, 2025 — *cited as*, not proven; the figure is methodologically contested and must never be stated as causal evidence that "point tools fail because they lack a world model") is multi-causal — change management, data quality, unclear ROI — and the most we can honestly say is *consistent with* the thesis that fragment-optimising tools cannot move costs and leakage that live in the **seams between stages**. A copilot speeding one underwriting step does not change the unit economics of a 12-touch loan. Only a stack that runs the whole lifecycle on one ledger can reallocate work across the operation *and* decide with lifetime/portfolio context — that mechanism is the inference, and it is labelled as inference, not borrowed from the statistic.

## 5. Why only Krim's architecture can ship it — the triad, sharpened against the critiques

The defensible position is **not** a better algorithm, and **not** that the underlying data is unreconstructable. It is a **data-completeness-going-forward** advantage made shippable by a validation gate. State it precisely, and state its limits:

> A world model of an *operation* is only learnable if **one runtime** runs **the whole lifecycle** and records **every action, the choice set it was selected from, the reasoning, and the outcome** on **one ledger**. **Pre-execution validation** is what makes that runtime safe enough to be *allowed* to run a regulated lender. **Sovereignty** lets it run inside the perimeter on data that never leaves. **One stack for customer-facing and back-office work** is what makes the ledger complete on *both* sides of the wall.

**The genuinely subtle and genuinely defensible part — and its limit.** A bank can ETL origination, servicing and collections into a unified event store; Snowflake/dbt/their own data team do exactly this. What a retrospective join **cannot** recover is the **rejected alternatives**: the prices/limits/contact-strategies that were *evaluated and not chosen, and why*. Legacy systems logged the chosen action, not the considered-and-rejected set. An action-conditioned model wants (state, choice-set-considered, action-taken, reasoning, outcome) tuples; retrospective joins give (state, action-taken, outcome) at best. **This is the real differentiator** — and the honest limit is that it **only holds going forward from instrumentation**. So the advantage is a **time-lead and an execution lead, not a platonic moat**: any vendor or in-house team can start logging choice sets and reasoning the day they decide to. The durable version of the claim is narrow and true: *retrofitting a pre-execution gate that captures the choice set into a decades-old core's action path, or bolting it onto a cloud point-tool that only sees one slice, is a multi-year, high-risk program for the two incumbent classes that own distribution — while it is a greenfield startup's 12–24-month build.* The moat candidate is the **combination** (gate + whole-lifecycle scope + on-record reasoning + rejected choice sets) compounding into proprietary data — and that combination is precisely the part that is **DIRECTION and empty until deployments exist**.

This substrate is what makes the off-the-shelf methods finally applicable: uplift/CATE, bandits, survival models and constrained RL are all bottlenecked by exactly the joint, attributed, cross-lifecycle, action-conditioned data that fragmented stacks do not produce. The differentiator is the **dataset and the runtime that produces it**, not the models.

**Two failure modes the literature names, and the honest version of Krim's answer:**

- **Model exploitation.** An optimiser left alone with an imperfect model finds and exploits its errors. v1 claimed the pre-execution gate "answers" this; the scientist reviewer was right that it does not — **it relocates and bounds it.** Cleanly separated: **(a) the hard pre-execution feasibility/compliance gate is a consequence-bounding floor** — it blocks catastrophic or non-compliant *actions* regardless of how wrong the internal model is, which is the property a bank needs to ship; **(b) conservative/uncertainty-penalised value estimation inside the planner (offline-RL-style pessimism: CQL/IQL-type penalties on out-of-support value) is the actual antidote to exploitation.** These are *different mechanisms* — a hard feasibility filter applied after optimisation is not the same as regularising the value function — and Krim must commit to **both**, not conflate them. (The "Nyāya as offline-RL pessimism" line in v1 was imprecise and is retired.) A real side effect to manage: a gate that systematically blocks certain action regions **censors the data further**, tightening the very selection bias that cripples counterfactual learning — which is why mandated exploration (§6.1) is a requirement, not a nicety.
- **Evaluation difficulty.** v1's "ledger-as-evaluation-harness" claim was circular for the queries that matter, and the reviewers were right. **On-ledger replay validates only on-policy, in-distribution outcomes — what happened when you did what you did.** The model's reason for existing is off-policy counterfactuals, whose outcomes were *never realised* and which replay can *never* confirm. So the harness is strongest exactly where the model needs no help and silent exactly where it makes its risky claims. The honest, narrower truth: the one ledger gives two things a competitor lacks — **(1) real-time on-policy calibration and drift detection** (genuinely better than an annual backtest), and **(2) the substrate to run gated, small-budget randomized interventions whose realised outcomes validate counterfactuals *locally*** — converting unverifiable off-policy evaluation into a partially-experimental program over time. Counterfactual validation therefore requires **deliberate experimentation plus conservative off-policy evaluation with reported variance (doubly-robust / FQE-style), plus explicit abstention outside support** — and low one-step or on-policy loss is **not** accepted as evidence of counterfactual or planning validity (Grimm et al. on value-equivalence; He et al. 2024).

**Why competitors structurally cannot match the *combination* (directional facts; vendor capability claims are from marketing, not repeated as fact):**

- **Incumbent cores** (Temenos, FIS, Fiserv, Jack Henry, Oracle; Backbase now markets an "AI-native banking operating system") own the system-of-record, but the ledger records the **transaction, not the reasoning or the rejected alternatives**, and the AI sits *beside* the core as audit-after governance. Re-architecting the action path of a decades-old core to insert a pre-execution gate everywhere is a multi-year, high-risk program.
- **Lending-AI point tools** (Upstart — holder of the CFPB's first AI no-action letter, 2017 — Zest AI, Scienaptic, Taktile, Ocrolus; TrueAccord/Prodigal in collections) each see **one slice** and many run as a cloud call *outside* the perimeter, so no sovereignty. **Positioning consequence:** "explainable underwriting" is **not** defensible whitespace — Upstart and Zest already generate Reg-B adverse-action notices and out-credential a pre-revenue claimant. Krim's whitespace is **lifecycle-wide proof + the substrate**, not decision-level explainability.
- **Big-bank in-house teams** have the best data and talent, but it lives in siloed systems never unified by action-plus-reasoning-plus-rejected-alternatives on one record; reconstructing a clean end-to-end behavioural model is a multi-year data-engineering program, and SR 11-7 governance slows every model into production. They build models; they do not build governed runtimes that gate actions across the whole lifecycle.
- **The academic frontier** (RL for credit-limit management, causal ML for credit, tabular foundation models) supplies the *ingredients* but runs in **simulators and offline backtests**, and RL's exploration ("explore" by denying or approving real applicants) is unacceptable on live credit without the safety gate and the controls in §6.1.

**The distribution problem — the unaddressed killer in v1, owned here.** The architecture argument does not, by itself, get a pre-revenue startup hired to run the most-examined, highest-blast-radius function a bank has. Incumbents own the core relationship, the integrations and the risk committee's trust; they do not need to copy the WLM — they need to ship a "good-enough" governed-AI module to a captive, already-integrated installed base. The only credible answer is a **narrow, low-blast-radius beachhead** (collections is the obvious wedge — clear ROI, outcomes that label in weeks, the TrueAccord/Prodigal comparison) plus a **land-and-expand path to lifecycle breadth**, *plus* a regulatory tailwind that makes incumbents' audit-after approach insufficient. This concedes that in a single-stage beachhead **Krim is temporarily a point tool too**, and the world-model claim is weaker until lifecycle breadth is real. That is a plausible go-to-market, not a refutation of the distribution disadvantage — and it should be stated as such.

**The regulatory hooks — stated at their true strength, not inflated.**
- **CFPB Circular 2022-03** holds that "the model is too complex" is **not** a cognizable defence under ECOA for failing to give specific adverse-action reasons (verifiable via consumerfinance.gov). This makes per-decision, applicant-level explainability **legally required** — but it does **not**, on its own text, mandate *pre-execution* validation; a bank can satisfy ECOA with post-hoc reason codes on an explainable model. **So drop any "pre-execution validation is legally mandatory" phrasing — it is not; explainability is.**
- The honest, stronger composite claim: **SR 11-7** (validate before use + ongoing monitoring), the **EU AI Act** high-risk credit-scoring regime (Art. 10 data governance, Art. 12 logging, Art. 14 human oversight; obligations phasing through 2 Aug 2026), and **FCA Consumer Duty**'s evidence-of-good-outcomes burden collectively push toward **ex-ante control plus continuous evidence**, which is the regime pre-execution validation most cleanly satisfies. The claim is *"the architecture best-aligned with the combined regime's intent,"* not *"the literal text of ECOA requires us."* And note the double edge the EU AI Act creates: a **censored, reject-biased ledger is an Art. 10 data-governance finding, not a strength** — the substrate's known bias must be documented and mitigated, not marketed as clean data.

## 6. The hard problems, and how Krim's design answers each — honestly, including where it cannot yet

**This section is the thesis. The architecture is only as credible as its answers here, and several answers are partial. Where there is no full answer, that is stated.**

### 6.1 Counterfactual identification — the marquee claim sits on the weakest ground

The intervention query "what would the declined applicant have done if approved" is **point-unidentified** under reject-inference: selection into approval is non-random and partly on *unobservables* (loan-officer soft information, prior policy), so conditional ignorability fails, and Heckman-style correction leans on exclusion restrictions the literature itself calls very weak. No amount of action-conditioned logging fixes this for the **never-approved** population — there is zero outcome support there, and a deep model will confidently extrapolate into the void (the OOD-overconfidence problem). Asserting recovered outcomes for never-approved protected-class applicants is not just fragile; under ECOA it is an **affirmative fair-lending liability**.

**Krim's answer (partial, and DIRECTION):** do not claim to *know* the counterfactual. Earn identification operationally — run **deliberate, gated, small-budget randomized experiments at the approval/price/limit/cadence margin** (within affordability and fair-lending gates), generating the exogenous variation observational data lacks, so realised outcomes validate the counterfactual *locally*. A runtime that gates and logs every action can do this; a frozen scorecard cannot. This converts an unidentified observational problem into a **partially-identified experimental** one, paired with calibrated uncertainty that widens sharply off-support and **explicit abstention (decline-to-decide) outside support**.

**Where there is no full answer:** randomized credit terms are themselves a fair-lending and Consumer-Duty minefield and require regulatory tolerance; experimentation generates support only **near the historical margin**, never for the deep never-approved population; and exploration by approving applicants you would have declined carries real cost and ethical weight. **Net: the strong "price the unseen population / unlock $5.7T" claim is not rescued — only a cautious, experiment-anchored, margin-local version is.** The deep-unseen population requires new data/origination, not model extrapolation.

### 6.2 Compounding rollout error and non-stationarity bound the horizon — and the tail is where it is needed most

Per-step model error compounds (super-)linearly over a rollout (the most robust negative result in model-based RL); a learned model is valid only on its training distribution; finance is non-stationary, and its decisive moments (2008, March 2020, a rate-regime break) are **off-distribution by construction**. So the model is most confident exactly where it is least correct — the tail it is sold to illuminate. A policy *change* is itself a deviation from the data-collection policy, the regime He et al. (2024) show even value-equivalent models evaluate poorly.

**Krim's answer:** confine trust to **short, near-distribution counterfactuals**; ship **calibrated epistemic uncertainty that widens off-distribution**; treat long-horizon/tail simulation as **uncertainty-aware scenario exploration under stated assumptions, not prediction**, reconciled to realised outcomes every period via the one-ledger backtest — explicitly **not a substitute for prescribed stress testing**. This is a real improvement on a frozen annual re-fit *if and only if* the uncertainty is honest.

**Where there is no full answer:** well-calibrated OOD uncertainty is an **unsolved frontier problem** — deep models are notoriously overconfident off-distribution — so this must be *engineered against and evidenced* (backtested calibration curves, OOD detection on the bank's own historical regime breaks, documented abstention/defer-to-human via Kupa), not assumed. Until shown, the tail-simulation pitch is DIRECTION.

### 6.3 Data sufficiency is a category problem, not a cold-start inconvenience

The lending label that matters (90+ DPD, charge-off, cure) matures over **12–60 months**, base rates are often low single-digit percent, and a single mid-size lender books on the order of tens of thousands of action-conditioned trajectories per year — most near-duplicates of the incumbent policy — over a combinatorial action space (price × limit × contact-sequence × restructure) with almost no coverage. This is a tiny, censored, slow-arriving, low-entropy sample relative to what learned-dynamics models consume. **Factual correction the reviewers caught: the v1 "~9 days / DreamerV3 ~9 days to mine diamonds" figure is wrong and is struck.** DreamerV3 (Hafner et al., *Nature*, 2025) is correctly cited as reaching the Minecraft diamond objective from scratch without human data, with sample-efficiency reported relative to prior methods; do not attach a fabricated wall-clock figure to it. The honest pitch is *"a model that **compounds** — every recorded operation improves future decisions,"* never *"learns from little data."*

**Krim's answer:** this is **not** a from-scratch RL problem — the structured-prior-plus-learned-residual stance (§2) drastically cuts the sample requirement; the value-equivalent lesson cuts *for* Krim (model only decision-relevant dynamics); short-horizon servicing/collections outcomes label in weeks, not years; and federation (Krim-Learn) pools across tenants to fight per-tenant sparsity.

**Where there is no full answer:** you **cannot** speed up a 36-month default label — some capabilities are simply gated by the speed of reality and must be paced behind surrogate/early outcomes with honest caveats; the combinatorial action-coverage gap means most counterfactual queries fall outside support; and **federation carries its own unsolved problems** (6.4, 6.6). A falsifiable commitment is owed: state the minimum trajectory volume/diversity and the time-to-parity at which a per-tenant model is expected to beat the bank's existing PD/LGD + scorecard stack on a held-out backtest. Until that is measured, "it compounds" is a hypothesis, not an asset.

### 6.4 A self-evolving model is *harder* to validate under SR 11-7 — and the gate is a control, not validation

Two category errors v1 flirted with, both corrected:
- **The pre-execution gate is a control, not model validation.** SR 11-7 requires conceptual soundness and *independent effective challenge*. Calling the gate "the embodiment of SR 11-7" is a category error; the gate **evidences compliance, it does not discharge validation.** Frame it as **a control on top of independent per-model-version validation, never a substitute** — and drop "the gate embodies SR 11-7."
- **Continuous compounding and per-version validation are in tension.** SR 11-7 demands reproducing any decision as it was made; a model that changes daily is a model-risk nightmare. The resolution is a **two-tier lifecycle**: *learn in shadow; promote only frozen, change-controlled, independently-validated checkpoints to the live path; every promotion triggers re-validation and an EU-AI-Act "substantial modification" check.* This means **"version" = a frozen release** — at which point the snapshot returns *for the live decision path*, by design, while learning continues in shadow. That is the honest reconciliation: not "continuous validation gives you SR 11-7 without the snapshot," but "continuous *learning* in shadow + *gated promotion* of frozen, validated versions to live."
- **"Marking its own homework."** One runtime that proposes, validates, monitors and learns is a concentration-of-function red flag under three-lines-of-defence. The answer must be explicit: **the bank's independent second line owns and change-controls the gate and the monitors, with authority to halt and override via Kupa.** Effective challenge has to be evidenced as *independent*, or a CRO sees the hole in the first meeting.

### 6.5 Fair lending is statistical and population-level — per-action gating structurally cannot catch it

This is the deepest governance gap, and v1 under-confronted it. **Disparate impact is an aggregate property; each individual action can be compliant while the population distribution discriminates.** A pre-execution gate over individual actions is *structurally incapable* of catching it — and a return-optimiser is exactly what finds the legal-looking proxies (geography, device, cashflow timing, merchant category) that reproduce protected-class-correlated patterns: redlining-by-proxy at machine speed. The empirical record refutes the optimistic prior directly: the **Relman Colfax fair-lending monitorship of Upstart** (final report, 2024) — the most rigorous independent audit of an AI underwriter to date — found statistically and practically significant adverse approval/denial disparities for Black applicants **even though the model passed proxy-variable and pricing-disparity tests.** A WLM is strictly more expressive and faster, so naively it *scales* this failure mode.

**Krim's answer (necessary, insufficient, and partly DIRECTION):**
- **Redefine the objective in writing.** An action that maximises lender NPV but fails an affordability or disparity test is **not a "constrained-optimal" action — it is a blocked action.** Borrower-welfare and disparate-impact metrics must be **first-class, audited outputs of every counterfactual rollout**, not side-constraints traded off at the margin. (This commitment is DIRECTION today and must be evidenced before any social claim is made.)
- **Add a population-level fair-lending harness that gates policies and model versions, not just actions** — adverse-impact metrics (with BISG/other proxy methods where protected-class data cannot be collected), and a **documented less-discriminatory-alternative (LDA) search** as a pre-promotion constraint. The one attributed ledger makes the disparity *measurable continuously* rather than at the next annual backtest — a genuine advantage over audit-after.
- **Make Krim-Learn's admission criterion fairness-aware.** "Patterns admitted above an effectiveness threshold" is, as written, a **bias-preserving rule** — historical effectiveness encodes historical disparity, so any disparity that was historically profitable is exactly what gets pooled. No pattern enters the federated loop unless it passes a disparate-impact and LDA screen, and "effectiveness" is redefined to include a fairness term.

**Where there is no full answer:** disparate-impact law has **no bright-line numeric safe harbour** (four-fifths is a screening heuristic); the LDA search the Upstart monitorship spent years on is an **unsolved methodological problem**; and in US credit, protected-class data often cannot legally be collected, so proxy methods are themselves contestable. The honest safety claim is **"demonstrably better-governed and continuously monitored,"** never "safe." And the validator covers only *specified* harms — a more capable optimiser puts more adversarial pressure on whatever the policy library forgot (specification gaming), which is why the action space is restricted to **validated Kriya primitives with human-in-command via Kupa for novel actions**, plus **continuous red-teaming of the validator set** with the ledger as detection substrate. "33 validators from Navya-Nyāya" is brand/IP and internal-consistency, **not a coverage proof** — and must never be presented as one.

### 6.6 Performativity, adversarial adaptation, and federated herding — a systemic-risk surface, not only a moat

A lending policy is **performative**: deploying it changes who applies (adverse selection), changes borrower behaviour (people game observable signals — Goodhart), and changes the data the model next trains on — so the i.i.d. assumption under "validated against held-out outcomes" is violated **by the model's own deployment**, and held-out validation on pre-intervention data does not bound post-intervention performance (reflexivity; Soros/Sornette). Fraud is an *adaptive adversary*, so the transition function depends on the policy — a non-stationary game, not a fixed MDP. Dyna's "every operation improves the model" guarantee assumes a stationary environment and adequate exploration; **under performativity and adversarial adaptation, naive compounding has no such guarantee** and can converge to a performatively-stable-but-globally-bad equilibrium, including self-confirming discrimination (decline a segment → never observe counter-evidence → grow more confident in declining). Worst of all, **Krim-Learn's cross-tenant pooling is a herding/synchronisation channel**: if many lenders' models learn the same de-risking trigger, they pull credit simultaneously — the microstructure of a credit crunch *and* a correlated mass disparate-impact event at once.

**Krim's answer (partial):** encode **mandated exploration floors and fairness constraints as validators that fire before action**; use the one ledger to make feedback loops and reflexive drift **detectable** (fragmented stacks cannot even see them); preserve **per-tenant policy heterogeneity**, default **opt-out for risk-sensitive patterns**, and **do not propagate credit-tightening triggers** across tenants; share only non-decisional artifacts and keep credit-decision parameters strictly per-tenant with local validation.

**Where there is no full answer:** performativity and adversarial non-stationarity are **open research problems with no clean solution**; observing drift is not preventing Goodhart signal decay; and **anonymized pooling of "effective patterns" is itself the synchronisation channel** — federation widens the disparate-impact and systemic-correlation surface even as it fights sparsity. **The unqualified tagline "Smarter after it acts" / monotonic-compounding framing is scientifically unsupportable under non-stationarity and must be qualified** (see §8). Treat Krim-Learn as a systemic-risk surface to be governed, not an unalloyed moat.

### 6.7 The validation gate is an engineerable pattern; the moat is prospective

"Validate before execute" (policy-as-code, an OPA/Rego-style gate in the action loop) is buildable by a well-funded greenfield rival in **12–24 months**; the Navya-Nyāya framing is brand differentiation, not technical lock-in; sovereignty is a deployment posture others can match **and one that slows Krim's own go-live** versus SaaS rivals. The durable advantage is being the **first layer designed in** (re-architecting an incumbent core's action path or a point tool's single-slice scope to insert a gate-plus-choice-set-capture is genuinely a multi-year program for the two classes that own distribution) **plus the operating record that accrues only by landing deployments first.** This is "architecturally correct and early" — a real *why-now/why-us* in a regulated market where switching costs and examiner trust compound — **not** an unassailable moat. Do not oversell "only Krim can" into "no one ever can." It is a race that compounds into a moat *if and only if* Krim lands lifecycle-breadth deployments before the gate is commoditised, against a real distribution disadvantage and the most risk-averse buyer in the enterprise.

### 6.8 Cold-start: the asset is currently prospective

There are **no disclosed deployments**, so there is currently **zero empirical evidence** for the core claims — no measured calibration, no demonstrated counterfactual validity, no evidence the cross-lifecycle signal is learnable at useful fidelity, no evidence federation beats per-tenant models, no horizon at which rollout error is shown to stay bounded. **Under SR 11-7, no ledger and no validation evidence effectively bars deploying the *learned model*** on the live decision path on day one. The claim that needs **no** deployment to be true is the **negative/architectural** one: *fragmented stacks structurally cannot produce attributed, cross-lifecycle, action-conditioned-with-rejected-alternatives tuples; a single validated runtime over one ledger can.* That is sound today. Everything downstream of it is **the open empirical bet this company is making** — and §1's register reflects that: not "the scientific object that *can* answer it," but "the object whose form matches the question; whether it answers it at useful fidelity in lending is the bet."

This is the boundary every page, deck and demo must hold. It is not a disclaimer to voice anxiously; it is the discipline that keeps the confident claims credible.

**Assertable today (real now):**
- **One attributed ledger + deny-by-default pre-execution validation** over allow-listed, credit-native Kriya primitives — proof before action, with every action and its rationale on one record. *(This is the shippable product and a real improvement on incumbent audit-after governance.)*

- **Whole-lifecycle scope as the architecture**, and **human-in-command supervision via Kupa**, with a second-line-ownable gate.
- The **negative architectural claim**: fragmented stacks cannot produce the substrate; one validated runtime can.

**DIRECTION / active research (real as intent, not as shipped capability):**
- The **high-fidelity simulating World Lending Model** and long-horizon/tail portfolio simulation as anything beyond uncertainty-bounded scenario exploration.
- **Counterfactual reject-inference as an access engine** beyond cautious, experiment-anchored, margin-local estimates with abstention — and *never* for the deep never-approved population.

## 8. Messaging spine — how to talk about this on krim.ai

Regulator-credible, no hype, deflationary where the science is thin and confident where the architecture is sound. The discipline: **lead with the architecture and the gap (which need no deployment), describe the world model as the dividend the architecture makes possible, and keep every forward claim in the language of construction.** Match HOUSE-STYLE: punchy hero, depth in glass cards, colour grammar lighting the load-bearing words (mint = validated/proof, cyan = thinking/proposed).

**What to lead with (true today):** *Banks decide with frozen, single-purpose models stitched across silos — and no record of why each action was taken or what was rejected. Krim runs the whole lending lifecycle on one ledger, and validates every action before it happens. That record is the thing no one else has — and the only foundation a model of how a lending operation behaves could ever be built on.*

**What to call the world model (honest framing):** not "MuZero for lending," not "we know what rejected applicants would have done." Rather: *the learned model of a lending operation that becomes possible once — and only once — every action, its reasoning and its alternatives live on one validated ledger.* The dividend of the architecture, built going forward, an active area of research.

**Designated verbatim taglines (preserve):** "The AI your regulator can read." — fully supported by the substrate + gate, today. "Validated before it acts. Smarter after it acts." — **keep, but the second clause is a forward claim; never let it imply *monotonic, autonomous* improvement.** It describes the compounding *intent*, voiced once and lightly, not a guarantee. Do not spin off a standalone unqualified "Smarter after it acts" as if compounding were proven under non-stationarity.

**Candidate lines (regulator-credible, hype-free — choose at site phase):**
1. **"One ledger. Every action proven before it happens — and remembered with its reasoning."** *(Leads with the substrate + gate; both true today.)*
2. **"The model of how your lending operation behaves can only be built on a record of how it behaved. We're building the record."** *(Deflationary, honest, makes the architectural bet the headline.)*
3. **"Proof before action. The intelligence is the dividend."** *(Keeps proof primary, the world model secondary and downstream — the correct order.)*
4. **"Validation isn't a report you read after. It's the gate the action clears before."** *(Sharpens the wedge vs. incumbent audit-after governance.)*
5. **"We don't claim to know what the applicant you declined would have done. We claim the only architecture that could ever responsibly find out."** *(Turns the causal-identification honesty into a credibility asset — the line a model-risk officer trusts.)*

**Lines to retire:** "access-unlock makes it a mission"; "unlock the $5.7T gap"; "see tail risk before it materialises"; "MuZero/JEPA for a lending operation"; any phrasing that the gate *is* SR 11-7 or that pre-execution validation is legally required. Each is the exact sentence a sharp CRO, scientist, or fair-lending examiner punctures first — and each has a stronger, narrower replacement above.
