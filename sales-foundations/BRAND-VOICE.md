# BRAND-VOICE.md

The Krim site speaks in a register that might be called executive-literary: short, declarative, structurally confident, with occasional moments of quiet formality. It is not casual and not technical — it speaks to senior buyers who answer to regulators and boards, without patronising them with jargon or softening claims with hedges. The designated taglines are the sharpest form of the voice: "The AI your regulator can read." The Sanskrit naming system is deployed with precision and restraint, treated as technical vocabulary rather than mysticism.

---

## Register

Neither purely executive nor purely technical — both at once. The site reads at the level of a brief to a chief risk officer: precise about mechanisms, confident about outcomes, never breathless. The company page thesis section is the register at its most literary: "The institutions that move the world's money and serve its citizens could never run on AI they couldn't prove, or risk what left their walls. So the most consequential work stayed manual — too important to automate, too costly to leave alone." The rest of the site is more compressed — headline, one qualifying line, glass cards.

HOUSE-STYLE §10 codifies this: "Confident, precise, quietly literary — sells the outcome and stays credible to a regulator."

---

## Sentence rhythm and length patterns

**The dominant pattern is short claim + single qualifier.** A headline makes a claim; a following sentence (often in a glass card) makes it precise.

Example: "Nothing runs until it has cleared the gate." (three-word subject + predicate). Then the qualifier: "Most actions clear. The ones that don't are flagged and handed to a person, with the rule that stopped them in plain words." (/platform/kendra)

**Two-sentence rhythm in body copy.** Most body paragraphs pair two short sentences rather than building a long one: "Lending runs on two workforces — the people who speak to customers and the people who keep the books — held apart by tickets, spreadsheets and hand-offs. Together they are 40–60% of what every loan costs." (/lending section 2)

**Em-dash as the preferred pause.** The site uses em-dashes to append a precise qualifier mid-sentence rather than building a subordinate clause: "Sovereignty is a commitment, not a deployment option." "Validation is the runtime, not a wrapper bolted on." "The work gets done, not just recommended."

**Short imperative headlines.** "Intelligent co-workers, not bots." "A finite vocabulary, endless combinations." "Plain language in. Governed work out." "Every customer, treated like the only one."

**Parallel structure.** When the site uses pairs, the rhythm is AB / AB: "Validated before it acts. Smarter after it acts." "Proof and improvement from one source of truth." "Safe to run, and sharper for having run."

**Sentence lengths:** headlines run 4–9 words. Body sentences run 15–30 words. Subheads and glass card callouts run 8–20 words. Mono labels (eyebrows) run 2–5 words. The shortest and highest-contrast copy is in glass callout cards: "Finite vocabulary. Infinite workflows."

## The Sanskrit naming register

The layer and module names (Nyāya, Kriya, Karta, Kendra, Kula, Kira, Kupa, Krimkar) are treated as proper nouns — capitalised, used without translation in most contexts, introduced with a descriptor in context where needed.

**How the site handles them:**
- First use on each layer page: eyebrow label gives the function ("The runtime," "The vocabulary," "The co-workers"), headline gives the Sanskrit name integrated into a claim
- Running text: used without translation, treated as known vocabulary once introduced
- Mono labels (IBM Plex Mono, uppercase): "KRIM-NYĀYA · VALIDATION" — module name first, English role second
- The company page explains the naming logic directly: "Our validator is Krim-Nyāya. It draws on Navya-Nyāya — the formal-logic tradition of Mithila, two thousand years of rigorous reasoning about what follows from what. Substance, not mysticism."

**Diacritics are used:** Nyāya (not Nyaya), Krim-Nyāya, Krimkar, Navya-Nyāya. HOUSE-STYLE explicitly permits "romanised Sanskrit with diacritics" but prohibits Devanagari script.

**The naming is treated as a differentiator, not an affectation.** The three Navya-Nyāya families (Pramāṇa, Doṣa, Yogyatā) are named on the homepage's PolicyChecks section and in documentation — they are technical vocabulary with operational meaning, not decorative Sanskrit.

- Provable, proven before it acts (approximately 6+ instances)
- Lifecycle (approximately 15+ instances)
- Operationally, in operational terms (approximately 5+ instances)
- Jurisdictions (approximately 10+ instances)

**The site avoids (from HOUSE-STYLE §10 and krim-content.md):**
- Revolutionary
- Cutting-edge
- Unlock
- Leverage
- Empower
- Game-changing
- Seamless
- Transformative
- Innovative
- Next-generation
- Powerful (appears once in /research as "A powerful world model, made safe enough to act" — in a framing context, not as a hollow adjective)
- Best-in-class
- End-to-end (used once as a section eyebrow "End to end" on /lending — as a factual descriptor, not as a phrase meaning "comprehensive")
- Holistic
- Robust (not used)

**The hedging vocabulary the site avoids:**
- "It helps teams..." (too soft — the site says co-workers "run" the operation, not "help")
- "Can be used to..." (the site makes direct claims about what the system does)
- "May include..." (avoided)
- "Potentially..." (avoided)
- "Aims to..." (avoided)
- Anxious disclaimers ("this is a research direction and not a shipped feature") — the site uses a single light "active area of research" touch; it does not stack disclaimers

## Do/Don't examples

These "do" lines are pulled verbatim from the site. The "don't" versions illustrate what the voice specifically avoids.

### 1. The validation claim

**DO (homepage, section 2, power 1):**
"Pre-execution validation — Wrong actions never reach a borrower. Every action is checked against your policy and the loan file before it happens — and lands on the record, in plain words you can read."

**DON'T:**
"Our advanced compliance engine helps ensure AI-generated actions are validated against relevant policies before they may potentially reach customers, helping to reduce regulatory risk."

*Why the don't fails: hedged ("may potentially"), passive ("are validated"), vague ("advanced"), uses "reduce" where the site says violations are "made structurally impossible"*

---

### 2. The workforce claim

**DO (homepage, section 2, power 2):**
"AI co-workers — The work gets done, not just recommended. A digital workforce that runs origination, underwriting, servicing and collections — end to end."

**DON'T:**
"Our AI-powered platform empowers your team to handle more work with less effort, featuring intelligent automation across the lending lifecycle."

*Why the don't fails: "empowers" is banned, "AI-powered platform" is generic, "handling more work" undersells running the whole operation, the structure is feature-led not outcome-led*

---

### 3. The sovereignty claim

**DO (trust page, hero):**
"KrimOS runs inside your perimeter — on-prem by preference, no foreign API in the loop. Customer data, model weights, orchestration and telemetry stay behind your wall. Nothing leaves."

**DON'T:**
"We offer flexible deployment options including on-premises solutions that help meet your data residency requirements."

*Why the don't fails: "flexible" is vague, "help meet" is hedged, "data residency requirements" is a compliance checkbox framing rather than the site's architectural commitment*

---

### 4. The audit trail claim

**DO (trust page, section 5):**
"Krim-Ledger streams every action, decision, prompt, output and validation to one immutable trail. An auditor can replay any decision deterministically — what happened, the policy that applied, and the validation result behind it. The record is built as the work runs, so an inspection that once meant days of reconstruction is answered the same afternoon."

**DON'T:**
"Our comprehensive audit logging solution captures key actions and decisions to support your regulatory reporting and compliance documentation needs."

*Why the don't fails: "comprehensive" is a hollow adjective, "key actions" is vague, "support your needs" is passive, the specific time comparison (days to an afternoon) disappears*

---

### 5. The learning claim

**DO (homepage, section 4, flywheel section):**
"One runtime runs the whole lifecycle and records every action with its reasoning on one ledger — so KrimOS builds a connected model of how lending actually behaves, and sharpens with every outcome. Validation lets it act; the record, kept inside your walls, lets it learn."

**DON'T:**
"Our machine learning capabilities continuously improve the system's performance over time by leveraging historical data from your lending operations."

*Why the don't fails: "leverage" is banned, "continuously improve performance" is vague and passive, the causal mechanism (validation enables learning because it produces attributed records) disappears*

---

### 6. The integration claim

**DO (homepage, section 8):**
"Fits the stack you already run. Sits on top of your core, LOS and LMS. No rip, no replace. 40+ connectors."

**DON'T:**
"KrimOS seamlessly integrates with your existing technology ecosystem through our robust connector library, enabling quick deployment without disrupting your current workflows."

*Why the don't fails: "seamlessly" is banned, "existing technology ecosystem" is corporate padding, "robust" is meaningless, the compression and rhythm of the original are lost*

## How claims are structured

**The site leads with the claim, then qualifies.** The headline or eyebrow states the outcome; the body copy gives the mechanism or the evidence. The structure is never "here is the context, here is the problem, here is why our solution addresses it" — it starts with the resolved state.

Examples:
- Headline: "Nothing runs until it clears the gate." Body: "Before any action fires, it passes through Krim-Nyāya — 33 validators that ask, in formal logic, whether this action is allowed to happen at all."
- Headline: "Eight modules make the guarantees hold." Body: "Nyāya and Learn get the attention; these are the engineering that makes them possible."
- Headline: "Today, the credit decision is yours." Body: "Karta-Risk and Karta-Decide segment portfolios, suggest the next best action, resolve conflicts and gate actions on the institution's own risk and fraud flags. That is the whole of their authority today."

The one exception is the challenge sections, which open with the problem to create contrast with the solution. But even there, the headline names the problem with precision rather than hedging: "The work is manual because the AI couldn't be proven." (/lending section 2)

## How the site handles uncertainty and direction vs. shipped capability

The site has a clear but light-touch protocol for claims about the World Lending Model and the AI underwriter:

**The pattern:** State the capability confidently, use present-tense framing ("sharpens with every outcome"), include one natural signal that this is a direction ("we are building," "the frontier of our research") — then don't stack disclaimers.

Examples:
- /platform/karta gold card: "Today, the credit decision stays with you. The AI underwriter we are building — the evolving, self-learning World Lending Model — earns its place by clearing the same validation gate as every other action."
- /research/world-lending-model close: "The World Lending Model is the frontier of our research, and it compounds with every validated outcome it records."
- /platform/kendra: Krim-Learn described as "A pattern only joins the shared library once it clears an effectiveness threshold of around 80%, and what is shared is anonymised, aggregated and opt-out. The system compounds; a baseline in the first quarter is materially beyond it by year two."

The lending page footnote: "Today the line is clear: Karta segment risk, suggest the next best action and gate on your own flags — the credit decision stays yours. The safe AI underwriter we are building — the World Lending Model — is the direction, and it will clear the same validation gate as every action that runs today." One sentence of context, no anxious stacking.

HOUSE-STYLE §10 codifies this: "Internal caveats — what's shipped vs. in research, what we can or can't claim — shape what we write; they are not voiced as anxious hedges. State the thing with confidence; where something is still being built, a single light 'an active area of research' touch is enough. Never stack disclaimers."
